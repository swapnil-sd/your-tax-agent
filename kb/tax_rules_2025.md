## Itemized Deductions

### SALT (State and Local Tax) Deduction
- Cap: $10,000 MFJ / $5,000 MFS
- Includes: state income tax + local income tax + property tax
- OR state/local sales tax (in lieu of income tax)
- Note: This cap may change — TCJA SALT cap was set to expire after 2025 but may be extended

### Mortgage Interest Deduction
- MFJ limit: interest on up to $750,000 of acquisition indebtedness
- MFS limit: interest on up to $375,000 of acquisition indebtedness
- Home equity loan interest: deductible only if used to buy, build, or improve the home
- Points on purchase: fully deductible in year paid
- Points on refinance: must be amortized over loan term
- When refinancing: unamortized points from original loan become fully deductible in year of refi
- If mortgage exceeds limit: pro-rate interest (deductible = total interest × limit / actual principal)

### Charitable Contributions
- Cash: up to 60% of AGI
- Appreciated property (held >1 year): up to 30% of AGI (FMV deduction, no capital gains)
- Donor Advised Funds: same rules as direct donations
- Documentation:
  - Under $250: bank/credit card statement
  - $250+: written acknowledgment from charity
  - $500+ non-cash: Form 8283
  - $5,000+ non-cash: qualified appraisal required

### Medical Expenses
- Deductible: amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Only unreimbursed expenses qualify

## Retirement Contributions (2025)

### 401(k) / 403(b)
- Employee limit: $23,500
- Catch-up (age 50+): additional $7,500
- Catch-up (age 60-63): additional $11,250 (new SECURE 2.0 provision)
- Total limit (employee + employer): $70,000

### Traditional IRA
- Contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)
- Deductibility: depends on MAGI and retirement plan participation
- MFJ, both have retirement plan: phase-out $126,000 - $146,000
- MFJ, only spouse has plan: phase-out $236,000 - $246,000
- MFS with retirement plan: phase-out $0 - $10,000

### Roth IRA
- Contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)
- Income limit (MFJ): phase-out $236,000 - $246,000
- Income limit (Single): phase-out $150,000 - $165,000
- Backdoor Roth: contribute to non-deductible Traditional IRA, then convert to Roth (no income limit)

### HSA (Health Savings Account)
- Requires HDHP enrollment
- Self-only: $4,300 / Family: $8,550
- Catch-up (55+): additional $1,000
- Contribution deadline: April 15 of following year
- Triple tax advantage: deductible, grows tax-free, tax-free withdrawals for medical

## Additional Medicare Tax (Form 8959)

| Filing Status | Threshold | Rate |
|---------------|-----------|------|
| MFJ | $250,000 | 0.9% on wages above threshold |
| MFS | $125,000 | 0.9% |
| Single | $200,000 | 0.9% |

- Applied to Medicare wages (W-2 Box 5) + self-employment income
- Employers withhold 0.9% on individual wages over $200,000 (regardless of filing status)
- Difference between actual tax and withholding is reconciled on Form 8959

## Net Investment Income Tax — NIIT (Form 8960)

| Filing Status | MAGI Threshold | Rate |
|---------------|---------------|------|
| MFJ | $250,000 | 3.8% |
| MFS | $125,000 | 3.8% |
| Single | $200,000 | 3.8% |

- Applied to lesser of: net investment income OR MAGI above threshold
- Net investment income includes: interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income, royalties
- Does NOT include: wages, self-employment income, Social Security, tax-exempt interest
- Schedule C income/loss does NOT reduce net investment income

## Capital Gains & Losses

### Netting Rules
1. Net short-term gains/losses within short-term category
2. Net long-term gains/losses within long-term category
3. If one category is net gain and other is net loss, offset against each other

### Loss Limitations
- MFJ: $3,000 maximum deduction against ordinary income
- MFS: $1,500 maximum
- Excess carries forward indefinitely

### Wash Sale Rule
- Cannot deduct loss if substantially identical security purchased within 30 days before/after sale
- Applies across ALL accounts (not just the account where the sale occurred)
- Disallowed loss is added to cost basis of replacement shares

## RSU (Restricted Stock Unit) Tax Treatment

### At Vesting
- FMV of shares at vesting is ordinary income (reported on W-2)
- Employer typically withholds shares to cover taxes ("sell to cover")
- Cost basis = FMV per share at vest date

### At Sale
- Gain/loss = sale proceeds - cost basis (FMV at vest)
- If sold same day as vest: gain ≈ $0 (sell-to-cover)
- If sold later: short-term (<1 year from vest) or long-term (>1 year from vest)

### 1099-B Reporting Issue
- Brokers often report $0 cost basis for stock plan shares
- 1099-B marked as "basis not reported to IRS"
- Taxpayer MUST report correct basis on Form 8949
- Form 8949: use Box B (short-term, basis not reported) or Box E (long-term, basis not reported)
- Column (f): adjustment code "B"
- Column (g): basis adjustment amount
- Without correction: income is double-taxed (once as W-2, again as capital gain)

## Residential Clean Energy Credit (Form 5695, Part I)

- Solar panels: 30% of cost (no cap)
- Battery storage: 30% of cost (must be at least 3 kWh)
- Solar water heating: 30%
- Geothermal heat pump: 30%
- Small wind energy: 30%
- Fuel cell: 30% (up to $500/0.5 kW)
- System must be placed in service during tax year (operational, not just installed)
- Non-refundable credit (can carry forward if exceeds tax liability)
- Available through 2032 at 30%, then steps down

## Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (Form 5695, Part II / Section 25C)

- Heat pump (HVAC or water heater): 30%, up to $2,000
- Insulation, windows, doors: 30%, up to $1,200
- Electrical panel upgrade (for solar/EV): 30%, up to $600
- Energy audit: 30%, up to $150
- Maximum total per year: $3,200
- Must meet Energy Star or similar certification
- Separate from solar credit (can claim both in same year)

## Qualified Business Income Deduction (Section 199A / Form 8995)

- 20% deduction on qualified business income
- Applies to: sole proprietorships, S-corps, partnerships, REITs, PTPs
- REIT/PTP dividends: Section 199A dividends (1099-DIV Box 5) qualify
- Income limits for full deduction:
  - MFJ: $383,900 - $483,900 (phase-out range for specified service businesses)
  - Single: $191,950 - $241,950
- Below phase-out: full 20% deduction regardless of business type

## Schedule C (Business Income/Loss)

### Hobby vs Business
IRS factors (no single factor is determinative):
1. Do you carry on the activity in a businesslike manner?
2. Do you depend on income from the activity?
3. Do your losses arise from circumstances beyond your control or from normal startup?
4. Have you changed methods of operation to improve profitability?
5. Do you have knowledge needed to carry on the activity as a business?
6. Have you made a profit in similar activities in the past?
7. Does the activity make a profit in some years?
8. Can you expect a future profit from the appreciation of assets?
9. Is this activity for recreation/pleasure?

Safe harbor: profit in 3 of 5 consecutive years (2 of 7 for horse breeding)

### Key Deductions
- Equipment: Section 179 deduction (full cost in year 1, up to $1,250,000 for 2025)
- Vehicle: standard mileage rate $0.70/mile (2025) OR actual expenses
- Home office: simplified method $5/sq ft, up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)
- Software, subscriptions, supplies: fully deductible
- Travel: transportation, lodging, 50% of meals (if business purpose)

### Self-Employment Tax
- 15.3% on net profit (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare)
- Social Security portion capped at $176,100
- If also W-2 employee: SS wage base reduced by W-2 Social Security wages
- Deduct 50% of SE tax as above-the-line adjustment

## Foreign Tax Credit

- Credit (Form 1116) vs deduction (Schedule A) — credit is almost always better
- Simplified method: if total foreign tax ≤ $300 (single) or $600 (MFJ), can claim directly on Form 1040 without Form 1116
- All foreign tax must be from passive category income (dividends, interest)

## New York State — Non-Resident (Form IT-203)

### Who Files
- Non-residents with NY-source income (wages earned in NY)
- File Form IT-203

### What's Taxable
- Only NY-source income (W-2 wages allocated to NY)
- Investment income is NOT NY-source (unless from NY real estate)

### Tax Rates (2025)
| Rate | Taxable Income (MFJ) |
|------|---------------------|
| 4% | $0 - $17,150 |
| 4.5% | $17,150 - $23,600 |
| 5.25% | $23,600 - $27,900 |
| 5.5% | $27,900 - $161,550 |
| 6% | $161,550 - $323,200 |
| 6.85% | $323,200 - $2,155,350 |
| 9.65% | $2,155,350 - $5,000,000 |
| 10.3% | $5,000,000 - $25,000,000 |
| 10.9% | $25,000,000+ |

### Key Rules
- NY income allocation: based on days worked in NY vs total work days
- W-2 Box 16 (NY state wages) is typically the correct allocation
- Do NOT allocate investment income to NY

## New Jersey — Resident (NJ-1040)

### Who Files
- NJ residents file NJ-1040 on worldwide income

### Tax Rates (2025)
| Rate | Taxable Income |
|------|---------------|
| 1.4% | $0 - $20,000 |
| 1.75% | $20,000 - $35,000 |
| 3.5% | $35,000 - $40,000 |
| 5.525% | $40,000 - $75,000 |
| 6.37% | $75,000 - $500,000 |
| 8.97% | $500,000 - $1,000,000 |
| 10.75% | $1,000,000+ |

### Key Rules
- **Property tax deduction:** NJ allows FULL property tax deduction (no $10K federal SALT cap)
- **Credit for taxes paid to other states:** Claim credit for NY taxes paid to avoid double taxation
- **NJ does NOT conform to many federal provisions:**
  - No Section 199A (QBI) deduction
  - Different treatment of capital gains (taxed as ordinary income in NJ)
  - No SALT cap on state return
- **NJ wage amount:** May differ from federal W-2 Box 1 (check W-2 Box 16 for NJ)

### Credit for Taxes Paid to Other States
- Claim on NJ-1040 Schedule A
- Limited to: NJ tax on the income taxed by the other state
- Cannot exceed actual tax paid to the other state
- Must have filed the other state's return first (file NY IT-203 before NJ-1040)
